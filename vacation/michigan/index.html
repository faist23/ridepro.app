Straits of MackinacThree slow days from St. Ignace, Michigan

Lighthouses and freighters, fudge and wooden boats, a car-free island and the Mighty Mac at golden hour — a relaxed northern-Michigan escape, mapped mile by mile.

June 30 – July 3, 2026 Base: St. Ignace (3 nights) From Columbus, OH · 490 mi
Welcome Aboard

A guide built for an unhurried pair of travelers.

This is a trip with nothing to prove and plenty to see — scenic drives that never feel rushed, waterfronts to linger on, lighthouses on the horizon, and small towns where the pace is set by ferry whistles and freighter horns. Everything here is arranged around your base in St. Ignace, with each day a loop that returns you to the water by evening. Use the chapters in order, or wander.

Daylight & Time Zone

Sunrise ~6:10 AM Sunset ~9:25 PM Zone Eastern (no change) Water ~58–64°F

You're this far north and at the western edge of Eastern Time, so July evenings stay bright past 10 PM — wonderful for after-dinner walks and late golden-hour photography. No clock change from Columbus.

Traveler Tip
Long northern dusk means you can have an unhurried dinner and still catch sunset over the bridge. Aim to be at a viewpoint by 9:00 PM.

Bugs in Early July

Manageable but real. Expect mosquitoes at dawn and dusk near woods and water, and stable ("biting") flies along beaches on warm afternoons. The worst of the spring black flies has usually passed. Breezy Mackinac Island and open decks are nearly bug-free; the marshy edges of Les Cheneaux are buggiest at dusk.

Traveler Tip
Pack a DEET or picaridin repellent and apply before the Les Cheneaux waterfront late in the day. A breeze is your friend.

Cell Coverage

Good in St. Ignace, on Mackinac Island, in Sault Ste. Marie, and in Cedarville/Hessel, plus along I-75, US-2, and M-134. Expect dead spots on forested back roads and remote channel edges. Verizon is strongest across the U.P.

Smart Prep
Download an offline map of the region before you go — handy on the M-134 stretches where signal drops.

Getting to the Island

The Ferry Guide

There's no bridge to Mackinac — only the boat, and it's a lovely 16 minutes.

Two companies cross from St. Ignace. They share a parent company now but run different boats from different docks. Either gets you there in about the same time; the choice is mostly about which dock and price you prefer.

Don't Miss
Take at least one under-the-bridge ("Mighty Mac") departure — it routes you directly beneath the Mackinac Bridge for the best photos of the trip. It adds about 10 minutes; worth every one.

Timing & Boarding

No reservations — tickets are valid for any departure that day, and a round-trip can be used from either St. Ignace or Mackinaw City. Buy online to skip the ticket window; arrive at the dock 45–60 minutes before your preferred boat in peak summer.

Suggested departures: cross over around 9:30–10:00 AM to land ahead of the day-trip rush; return on a 4:30–6:00 PM boat after the crowds thin.

Best Seats & Comfort

The upper deck wins for views and photography — the bridge, the island skyline, passing freighters — but it's windier, cooler, and catches spray. The enclosed lower deck is warmer and steadier.

Traveler Tip
Prone to motion sickness? It's only ~16 minutes. Sit mid-ship on the lower deck, face forward, keep your eyes on the horizon, and bring ginger chews. Rough days are rare in the sheltered straits.
Parking
Leave your car in the St. Ignace day lot (~$15) — you won't need it on the island, which has had no cars since 1898. St. Ignace parking is paid on-site, not online.
01
Tuesday, June 30

The Drive North

Columbus → St. Ignace · ~490 mi · arrive in time for sunset

Today is mostly windshield time, but it's the good kind — a steady climb north as the land turns to pine and the air turns cool. Break it up with one proper stop, cross the Mighty Mac in the late afternoon, and let the first evening be slow.

Departure & Route

Roll out of Columbus by 8:00 AM to arrive with daylight to spare. Take I-75 to Toledo, then US-23 north to Flint to dodge Detroit, rejoining I-75 the rest of the way to the bridge.

Driving Time
Columbus → Frankenmuth ~3h50m · Frankenmuth → Mackinaw City ~2h45m · over the bridge to St. Ignace ~15 min.

Rest Stops

Clean, reliable breaks: the Michigan Welcome Center just over the line, the Birch Run / Frankenmuth area, the Higgins Lake–Grayling rest area, and Indian River before the bridge.

The Roadside Stop Worth Making

Two miles off I-75, Frankenmuth — "Michigan's Little Bavaria" — is tailor-made for a relaxed midday break: a covered bridge over the Cass River, flower-lined streets, riverside gardens, and Bronner's, the world's largest Christmas store. It's the natural lunch point, roughly the trip's midway mark.

Don't Miss
Frankenmuth's famous family-style chicken dinners at Zehnder's or the Bavarian Inn are an institution. Time your arrival for ~noon and stretch your legs along the river afterward.

Arrival Checklist · St. Ignace (~4:30 PM)

  • Check in and unload; confirm tomorrow's ferry plan
  • Scout the ferry docks on N. State Street
  • Walk the Huron Boardwalk before dinner
  • Pick a sunset viewpoint for ~9:00 PM
  • Buy ferry tickets online for the morning
  • Reserve or note a waterfront dinner table

Evening Walk & Sunset

Stroll the Huron Boardwalk along the marina, with interpretive signs and views across to Mackinac Island and the lighthouses. For sunset, head to Bridge View Park / Straits State Park beneath the Mackinac Bridge, or the Father Marquette National Memorial — both face the bridge as the sky goes gold around 9:25 PM.

Dinner Tonight

Keep it easy and on the water: The Galley or the Mackinac Grille for a waterfront deck, or the classic drive-in burgers at Clyde's. Whitefish is the local specialty — try it at least once this trip.

Wednesday, July 1

Mackinac Island

A full car-free day · ferry over · carriage, fort, fudge, and the Grand

Give the island the whole day. It's small enough to feel and large enough to surprise, and the rhythm — clip-clop of hooves, ferry whistles, the smell of fudge on the breeze — does the relaxing for you.

The Crown Jewel

Mackinac Island

Time neededA full day, 6–8 hrs
Best timeLand by 9:30 AM, before the rush
DifficultyEasy–moderate; some hills & stairs
RestroomsMarquette Park, Fort, restaurants
ParkingNone — leave the car in St. Ignace
AccessibilityFlat downtown; carriages accessible

Why it's worth it

A genuine step back in time: no cars, only horses, bicycles, and footpaths, set on a limestone island ringed by Victorian cottages and Lake Huron blue. It rewards exactly your pace — gardens, porches, harbor benches, and long views.

History

Sacred to the Anishinaabe and later a fur-trade hub and 1780s British fort, the island banned automobiles in 1898. Roughly 80% of it is Mackinac Island State Park — Michigan's first, established in 1895.

What makes it unique

The only Michigan "highway" with no motor vehicles (M-185) rings the shore for 8.2 flat miles. Fudge has been made here since the 1880s — day-trippers are affectionately called "fudgies."

Photography

The harbor with Round Island Lighthouse beyond; the Fort's overlook of the Straits; Arch Rock in morning light; the Grand Hotel's porch and gardens; horses on Main Street.

The Landmarks

Main Street & the Harbor. The island's lively heart — bike liveries, shops, and fudge kitchens with their copper kettles in the windows. Flat and walkable end to end in about ten minutes.

Marquette Park. A green lawn of flower beds below the fort walls, with plenty of benches — the best spot to simply sit and watch the harbor.

Mission Church (1829). The oldest surviving church building in Michigan, a white New England–style chapel near Mission Point. A quiet, photogenic stop.

Arch Rock. A natural limestone arch curving 146 feet above the lake on the island's east side — reachable by carriage, bike, or a short walk and stairway.

Historical Fact
Fort Mackinac (1780). Costumed interpreters, cannon and rifle firings, and 14 historic buildings on the bluff. Its Tea Room — run by the Grand Hotel — serves lunch with the finest harbor view on the island.
Don't Miss
The Grand Hotel. Walk the world's longest porch (660 ft, 1887). Non-guests pay a small grounds admission (~$10). Afternoon tea is a treat; note the evening dress code after 6 PM.
On Foot
Carriage tours are the easy way to cover Arch Rock, Surrey Hills, and the Grand in one relaxed loop — the island's livery is the largest horse-and-buggy operation in the world, and the tours run rain or shine.

Fudge Strategy
Save fudge-shopping for after 4 PM — the kitchens make fresh batches all day, and you'll skip the midday queues. Boxes travel home well in a cooler.

On Foot: Distances & Rest

Main Street is about half a mile end to end; the Fort is a short (steepish) climb or ramp; Arch Rock is roughly a mile out via a gentle path. Benches abound — Marquette Park, the harbor front, the Fort overlook, and the Mission Point lawn are the best sit-downs.

On Foot
Want the famous shoreline loop? It's 8.2 flat miles by rented bike, about 1.5–2 hours at an easy pace, with Arch Rock and British Landing along the way.

Crowd-Avoidance & Photos

Take an early ferry; do Main Street and fudge before 11 and after 4; spend the busy midday on the quieter east bluff and Mission Point. Weekdays beat weekends, and the July 4th buildup means earlier is better all around.

Rainy day? Thursday, July 2

Soo Locks & Les Cheneaux

A clean loop · freighters by morning, wooden boats by afternoon · ~120 mi, no backtracking

Today is a triangle: north to the Soo to watch giant lakers lock through, lunch in Sault Ste. Marie, then south to the hushed channels of Les Cheneaux before easing home along the Lake Huron shore. About 2.5 hours of driving spread across an easy day — and not a single boat ticket required.

Morning · The Soo Locks

Engineering on the St. Marys

Soo Locks

Time needed1–1.5 hrs at the deck
Best timeLate morning; time a freighter
DifficultyEasy, flat, paved
RestroomsInside the visitor center
ParkingMetered on Portage Ave (bring coins/app)
AccessibilityLower deck & center accessible; upper via stairs

Why it's worth it

You stand a few feet from 700- and 1,000-foot freighters as they're raised and lowered between two Great Lakes — a free, genuinely awe-inspiring show, and a perfect no-boat alternative to a cruise.

History

Opened in 1855, the locks let ships bypass the rapids of the St. Marys River. Today they're operated by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers and remain among the busiest locks on earth.

How the locks work

Gravity does it all. The chamber bridges the ~21-foot drop between Lake Superior and the lower river; ~22 million gallons flow in or out by gravity alone — no pumps. A ship enters, the gates close, the water level changes, and it sails on.

Photography

From the upper open deck you shoot straight down into the chamber as a freighter rises. Morning light favors the south-facing rail. Watch for 1,000-footers like the Paul R. Tregurtha.

What You'll See

Expect a parade of working freighters — 1,000-footers like the Paul R. Tregurtha (the longest on the lakes at 1,013 ft), the Edwin H. Gott, and the James R. Barker, plus the occasional ocean-going "salty" and tug-and-barge. Most pass through the Poe Lock, the only one big enough for the giants.

Photography Tip
Stand on the upper open deck and shoot down the rail as a freighter fills the frame. Get there before a scheduled vessel and you'll watch the whole rise from start to finish.

Timing & the Visitor Center

The show depends on traffic, so time it: call the freighter hotline (906) 202-1333 or check the MarineTraffic app for ships in the river. The free visitor center has a schedule board, a working model, and the history of the locks.

Practicalities
Free admission. Use metered street parking on W. Portage Ave (bring quarters or the app); a quick bag check at the gate. No pets or bikes inside.

Afternoon · Cedarville, Hessel & Snows Channel

Les Cheneaux Islands

Why it's worth it

Thirty-six wooded islands shelter a maze of glassy channels dotted with classic wooden boats — a quiet, postcard corner of Lake Huron that few visitors reach. Pure small-town waterfront calm.

History

"Les Cheneaux" (the channels) has been a boating haven for over a century. Cedarville's Mertaugh Boat Works was the first Chris-Craft dealership in the country, and the area still celebrates its heritage at August's famous Antique Wooden Boat Show in Hessel.

What makes it unique

Sheltered water so calm it reflects the pines — ideal for the slow scenic drive along M-134 and Snows Channel, with the islands floating just offshore.

Photography

Wooden boats at the Hessel and Cedarville marinas; channel reflections at golden hour; great blue herons standing in the shallows.

The Two Villages

Cedarville is the larger of the two — a working marina, the Les Cheneaux maritime and historical museums, and The Islander for a drink by the water. A few miles west on M-134, tiny Hessel centers on its marina (home of the wooden boat show), with a handful of boutiques and the Hessel Bay Inn for whitefish. Both have a marina-side coffee or ice-cream window for a slow afternoon treat.

Driving Time
St. Ignace → Soo 50 min · Soo → Cedarville 50 min · Cedarville → Hessel 10 min · Hessel → St. Ignace 35 min.

Wildlife & the Scenic Drive

The channels are a quiet birding haven — watch for common loons, great blue herons, bald eagles, ospreys, and sandhill cranes, with otters and beavers in the shallows. Bring binoculars. Drive M-134 slowly and use the shoreline pull-offs; the water is often mirror-calm.

Photography Tip
For lighthouse photos, the area's classic lights sit around the Straits — Round Island (seen from yesterday's ferry) and Old Mackinac Point in Mackinaw City. Here, the view is the islands themselves floating on still water.

Back in St. Ignace for Dinner

Return along the Lake Huron shore by early evening. Reward the day with whitefish and a water view at the Mackinac Grille or The Galley, or keep it casual at Clyde's. You'll still have daylight for one more boardwalk stroll.

04
Friday, July 3

Homeward

One last bridge photo, a souvenir, and the long road south

No need to rush the morning — but with July 4th weekend underway, an earlier start keeps the southbound traffic light. Take your bridge pictures in the clean morning light, grab the last of the fudge, and point the car home.

Breakfast

Start at Java Joe's for coffee and a hearty plate, or the old-fashioned soda-fountain charm of Bentley's B-N-L Café on the main drag.

Bridge Overlook

Make a final stop at Bridge View Park / Straits State Park — the U.P. side gives the postcard view of the Mackinac Bridge, and morning light is ideal. The Father Marquette National Memorial is right there too.

Souvenir Stop

Last fudge for the cooler, a bag of Mackinac Island Coffee, and local maple or cherry treats from the downtown St. Ignace shops.

The Drive & Lunch Stop

Reverse the route south. Break the trip with lunch back in Frankenmuth, or at Gaylord closer to the bridge. The Birch Run outlets make an easy leg-stretch if you want one.

Driving Time
~7.5 hrs to Columbus. With the holiday weekend starting, aim to clear the bridge by mid-morning.

Through the Lens

The Photography Guide

Where and when to point the camera · long northern light is on your side

Sunrise (~6:10 AM)

The St. Ignace boardwalk and East Moran Bay face east over Lake Huron. On Mackinac, the quiet east bluff and Mission Point catch first light on the water.

Sunset (~9:25 PM)

Bridge View Park / Straits State Park puts the Mackinac Bridge against a gold sky. The St. Ignace marina works too — and the long dusk gives you time after dinner.

Lighthouses

Round Island from the ferry crossing; Old Mackinac Point in Mackinaw City; distant St. Helena Island light west of St. Ignace along US-2.

Photography Tip
The Bridge. Shoot from beneath at Straits State Park for a dramatic up-the-cables angle, or from the ferry on an under-the-bridge departure.
Photography Tip
Arch Rock. Morning light from the overlook; include the lake through the arch for depth.
Photography Tip
Freighters. Soo Locks upper deck — fill the frame as the ship rises; a fast shutter freezes the churning water.
Photography Tip
Harbors & wooden boats. Mackinac's harbor with Round Island beyond; the Hessel and Cedarville marinas at golden hour.
Photography Tip
Wildflowers. Roadside lupines linger into early July along US-2 and M-134 — shoot low with the water behind.
Gear That Earns Its Space
Pack a polarizer to cut glare off the water and deepen those Lake Huron blues, and a small tripod for the late bridge sunsets. Golden hour up here runs long — roughly 8:15–9:25 PM.
